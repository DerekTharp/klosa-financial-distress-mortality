# Social Science & Medicine Editorial Panel Reviews

Prepared on 2026-03-25 from the Codex review session. These notes summarize 10 editor-style reviews focused on what it would take to make the manuscript ready for *Social Science & Medicine*.

## Recommended SSM Office

Most reviewers converged on the **Social Epidemiology** office as the best fit after revision.

Current official SSM scope checked during this session:
- SSM publishes social science research on health intended to be of broad interest to general social science readers.
- The Social Epidemiology office welcomes papers on social distributions and determinants of health that engage richly with social conditions and processes in relation to health and center population-level inferences.

## Editor 1: Editor-in-Chief

**Decision**
- Desk reject for now: interesting topic, but in its current form it reads as a technically dense, Korea-specific epidemiology paper rather than a broadly interesting social science paper for SSM.

**Required Changes**
1. Reframe the paper around a general social-science question, not just a Korean cohort result.
2. Simplify the methods story. Too much of the main text reads like a methods paper rather than a broad SSM contribution.
3. Temper causal and policy language.
4. Fix or explicitly justify the measurement-comparison problem between the subjective and objective indicators.
5. Broaden the discussion so it travels beyond Korea and speaks to general social science readers.

**Best SSM Positioning**
- The study shows that among older adults in South Korea, perceived economic insecurity predicts mortality beyond objective resources, offering a theoretically important test of whether health is shaped more by material deprivation or by socially mediated perceptions of deprivation.

**Suggested Office**
- Social Epidemiology.

## Editor 2: Social Epidemiology

**Scope**
- Yes, but only after recentering. The topic is clearly social epidemiology, but as written it reads more like a measurement/causal-model comparison paper than a paper about social conditions and population health.

**What Would Make It Attractive**
1. Reframe around social stratification and welfare context, not psychometrics.
2. Choose one primary inferential story.
3. Treat time-varying models as secondary, not co-equal proof.
4. Replace strong causal language with population-level association language.
5. Make measurement asymmetry a limitation, not a centerpiece.

**What To Cut Or Demote**
1. Demote MSMs.
2. Compress the long neuroticism bias-analysis discussion.
3. Demote wealth shocks.
4. Demote the Basic Pension interaction analysis to supplement or a brief contextual note.
5. Move subgroup, PH, cut-point, spline, and complete-case details to the appendix unless central.

**Best Title Direction**
- `Economic insecurity and mortality in later life: a 16-year national cohort study from South Korea`
- Or: `Subjective economic dissatisfaction, objective income, and all-cause mortality in the Korean Longitudinal Study of Aging`

## Editor 3: Medical Sociology

**Main Critique**
- The sociological contribution is still more implied than developed.

**What Is Missing**
- A clearer theory of how financial distress is socially produced and socially interpreted.
- More attention to the institutional and relational side: welfare-state design, stigma/deservingness, intergenerational obligation, classed expectations of adequacy, and life-course accumulation.

**Suggested Anchors**
- Pearlin stress process model
- Bourdieu on capital, habitus, symbolic violence
- Dannefer on cumulative disadvantage
- Welfare-regime and familism scholarship
- Goffman on stigma and shame for the discordant groups

**Advice On Korea**
- Use Korea as a structured institutional case, not as a thin “East Asian culture” example.
- The strongest Korea-specific material is the obligatory-supporter rule, low welfare take-up, Basic Pension expansion, high out-of-pocket costs, and the cohort’s exposure to the 1997 crisis.

**Suggested Office**
- Still Social Epidemiology, not Medical Sociology, unless the theory contribution is deepened substantially.

## Editor 4: Health Policy

**Bottom Line**
- Not policy-ready as a primary policy manuscript.
- Yes as a social epidemiology manuscript with policy implications.

**Claims To Soften**
- “Income interventions matter” should become “income changes are associated with mortality.”
- Basic Pension and financial counselling suggestions should be hypotheses, not recommendations.
- Do not imply the Basic Pension expansion reduced distress; the supplemental analyses point in the other direction.

**What Would Improve Policy Relevance**
1. Reframe around welfare-state and social protection design, not just exposure-outcome association.
2. Bring the Basic Pension period into the analytic spine if you want stronger policy relevance.
3. Add a more focused policy analysis if feasible, such as age 65+ eligibility or a cleaner pre/post interaction.
4. Put Korea in a comparative policy frame with OECD pension design and out-of-pocket health costs.

**Role Of Policy In The Paper**
- Secondary in the current version.

## Editor 5: Health Psychology

**Bottom Line**
- Not a strong Health Psychology fit as written.

**Could It Fit There?**
- Only with substantial reframing around appraisal, relative deprivation, perceived control, and culturally patterned meaning.

**Claims That Are Too Loose**
- “Chronic economic dissatisfaction”
- “Distinct temporal pathways”
- Personality-confounding statements that read too definitive
- Psychological mechanism language that is plausible but unmeasured
- Intervention language around counselling targeting perceived economic security

**Advice**
- Avoid this office unless doing a serious theory rewrite.

## Editor 6: Health Economics

**Bottom Line**
- Bad fit for Health Economics as written.

**Why**
- The paper is not really about resource allocation, health financing, or economic decision-making.
- It reads as a single-country cohort association paper rather than an economics paper.

**What Would Need To Change**
- Make the paper about transfer design, pension generosity, income volatility, consumption smoothing, or policy variation.
- Turn the Basic Pension material into the empirical core.
- Use quasi-experimental or stronger policy variation if possible.

**Advice**
- Do not chase the Health Economics office unless redesigning the paper substantially.

## Editor 7: Medical Anthropology

**Main Read**
- The Korea framing is stronger on institutions than on culture.

**What Works**
- Korea as a site with high elder poverty, universal coverage plus substantial out-of-pocket costs, means-tested welfare with the obligatory-supporter rule, and changing pension arrangements.

**What Overreaches**
- “South Korea is an ideal setting for this test”
- Any claim implying full isolation of the psychosocial pathway
- The `hyo` passage when used as explanation rather than hypothesis
- Sweeping statements about the 1997 financial crisis without tight grounding

**Advice On Korea**
- Present Korea as a rapidly aging welfare state in transition.
- Foreground institutions, stratification, and historical change.
- Treat filial norms as one possible interpretive layer, not the main mechanism.

**Advice On `hyo` Discussion**
- Cut it from the main explanatory argument, or reduce it to one cautious sentence framed as a hypothesis the study cannot test.

## Editor 8: Health Geography

**Bottom Line**
- There is no strong spatial or geographic analysis to elevate in the strict sense.

**What Is Useful**
- Korea as “place as institutional context,” not as a spatial-analysis setting.

**What To Avoid**
- Do not imply neighborhood, regional, or GIS-style place effects that the data cannot support.
- Do not let Korea become a generic backdrop for a portable psychological finding.
- Do not detach the psychosocial reading from the policy environment and welfare architecture.

**Useful Macro-Structural Framing**
- Korea as a rapidly aging post-industrial society
- Korea as a welfare regime in transition
- Korea as a context where family expectations and public protection jointly shape economic insecurity
- Korea’s means-tested assistance rules as an example of policy design shaping lived distress

## Editor 9: Quantitative Methods / Conceptual Rigor

**Single Biggest Coherence Problem**
- The paper treats differences across model classes as if they identify mechanism. Baseline Cox, time-updated Cox, and MSM do not jointly establish distinct causal pathways.

**Safest Claim**
- In KLoSA, subjective economic dissatisfaction was more consistently associated with all-cause mortality than the objective indicators in conventional adjusted Cox models; in time-varying models, low income remained associated after joint adjustment while dissatisfaction attenuated.
- This is an associational finding, not evidence of distinct causal pathways.

**Recommended Analysis Hierarchy**
1. Jointly adjusted baseline Cox model as primary
2. Discordance analysis as descriptive secondary evidence
3. Time-varying Cox as robustness/sensitivity
4. MSM in supplement, or without strong causal interpretation unless balance improves

**Need Another Analysis?**
- Yes, if retaining pathway language. Best next step: within-person/between-person decomposition or a materially better balanced MSM.

## Editor 10: Aging / Inequality / Family Ties

**Main Editorial Move**
- Shift the paper from “which financial measure predicts mortality?” to “what does economic security mean in later life when kin support is thinning and public protection is incomplete?”

**What Makes It Contemporary For SSM**
- Lead with the social problem, not the modeling.
- Treat subjective economic dissatisfaction as a socially meaningful indicator of insecurity, status, and relational strain.
- Make the contemporary relevance explicit: who protects older adults when family support no longer functions as the default safety net?

**What To Retain**
- Keep the weakened-filial-support framing.
- Use family expectations as context shaping meaning, not as a tested mechanism, unless directly measured.

**What To Rewrite**
- Downplay the horse race between objective and subjective indicators.
- Recast the introduction around economic insecurity as a relational and institutional experience shaped by family change, welfare-state adequacy, and cohort history.
- Compress technical adjudication and let the discussion do more conceptual work.

**Suggested Abstract Opening Sentence**
- `In rapidly aging South Korea, older adults’ economic security is shaped as much by thinning family support and uncertain public protection as by household income and assets.`

## Cross-Editor Consensus

The strongest SSM version would:

1. Target the **Social Epidemiology** office.
2. Reframe the paper around **economic insecurity in later life** rather than a horse race between measures.
3. Use the **jointly adjusted baseline Cox model** as the primary analysis.
4. Demote or compress the **time-varying, MSM, neuroticism-bias, wealth-shock, and policy-evaluation** side stories.
5. Treat Korea as an **institutional and historical case**, not a cultural essence.
6. Keep policy implications **secondary and cautious**.
7. Remove or sharply soften the **distinct temporal pathways** claim.

## Short Recommended Positioning Statement

Using a national Korean aging cohort, this study shows that perceived economic insecurity is not reducible to income or assets and remains associated with mortality in later life, highlighting how economic hardship is socially appraised within changing welfare and family systems.
