# SSM Final Polish Panel

**Date:** 2026-03-25  
**Manuscript reviewed:** `submission_ssm/manuscript.md`  
**Question posed to panel:** not “is the science ready?” but “what exact presentation and polish changes would make the current manuscript feel submission-ready at SSM without changing meaning?”

## Panel Composition

1. SSM editor-in-chief / desk-screen lens
2. SSM social epidemiology handling editor
3. Quantitative reviewer focused on presentation hierarchy
4. Aging / inequality reviewer focused on story and memorability
5. Line editor / academic prose reviewer
6. Final-submission reader looking for anything that would still catch the eye in a bad way

## Consensus

**Verdict:** `6 of 6` said **one last polish pass**, not a substantive revision.

The panel’s overall view was:

- the manuscript is **very close**
- the science is **stable and appropriately restrained**
- the main result is **easy to find**
- the remaining work is almost entirely **presentation-level**

No reviewer asked for:

- new analyses
- a new title
- a different journal strategy
- a change in the scientific meaning
- stronger causal language

## What SSM Reviewers Think of the Current Version

They think the current manuscript is **already good enough to look like a real SSM submission**. What still keeps it from feeling completely finished is not the study design. It is the last layer of prose and editorial finish.

The broadest shared judgment was:

> The manuscript is credible, readable, and well aligned internally, but the prose still over-explains its own caution and occasionally lets secondary analyses compete too much with the main answer.

## Shared Strengths

Across the panel, the strongest parts of the current version were:

1. **Clear narrative arc**
   The paper moves cleanly from theory and setting to the baseline joint model, the discordance analysis, and then time-varying/sensitivity checks.

2. **Appropriate inferential restraint**
   Reviewers consistently liked that the manuscript avoids overclaiming causality.

3. **Memorable empirical hook**
   The discordance finding remains the most memorable piece of evidence:
   dissatisfaction despite adequate income predicts higher mortality, while low income without dissatisfaction does not.

4. **Strong Korea context**
   The Korea institutional setting gives the paper a real reason to exist as more than a generic cohort study.

5. **Internal alignment**
   The abstract, tables, and discussion are broadly aligned around the same result hierarchy.

## Shared Remaining Weaknesses

The same handful of presentation issues came up repeatedly:

1. **The Discussion is still a little overqualified**
   Reviewers repeatedly said the prose explains its own caution too often.

2. **The baseline result and time-varying result still compete slightly**
   The paper intends the baseline joint model to be the main answer, but the time-varying discussion still draws more attention than it should.

3. **Some interpretation still leaks into Results**
   A few result paragraphs interpret too quickly rather than letting the estimates land first.

4. **There is some terminology drift**
   Reviewers noticed a mix of:
   - `economic insecurity`
   - `economic dissatisfaction`
   - `economic satisfaction`
   - `econ sat`
   - `HH income`

5. **Some prose still feels over-managed**
   Not “AI slop,” just slightly too self-conscious in the way it restates caveats.

## Exact Changes The Panel Most Agreed On

These were the highest-consensus polish edits.

### 1. Make the baseline joint model the unmistakable centerpiece

What they want:

- In the Abstract, lead with the baseline joint model.
- Present the discordance result as the memorable supporting pattern.
- Mention the time-varying result more briefly and more clearly as secondary.

Why:

- The current abstract is good, but still gives the mixed time-varying result slightly too much narrative space.

### 2. Trim repeated caution in the Discussion

What they want:

- Keep the current caveats.
- Stop repeating them in slightly different language.

High-priority targets:

- the opening Discussion paragraph
- 4.1 appraised experience
- 4.4 personality / alternative explanations
- 4.5 limitations
- 4.6 conclusion

Why:

- The paper is now credible enough that it does not need to keep re-proving its restraint.

### 3. Shorten or neutralize interpretation in Results

The panel repeatedly flagged:

- the income interpretation in 3.2
- the mediation/independence clause in 3.3
- the stacked caution in 3.6

The shared preference:

- report the result first
- interpret it once, briefly
- let the fuller explanation live in the Discussion

### 4. Standardize the terminology

This was a smaller point, but it came up often enough to matter.

The cleanest recommendation was:

- in prose, use `economic dissatisfaction` as the primary subjective-exposure label
- keep `economic insecurity` as the broader paper-level frame if you want
- avoid drift back and forth within the same section
- expand `HH income` and `econ sat` where possible in journal-facing text and labels

### 5. Tighten the final Conclusion

The panel liked the current conclusion, but wanted it to land more cleanly.

Their preferred closing logic:

1. baseline joint model is the primary finding
2. discordance is descriptive support
3. time-varying and treatment-weighted analyses are mixed and secondary
4. economic dissatisfaction is a risk marker, not causal proof

## Lower-Priority / Reviewer-Specific Suggestions

These came up, but not as full-panel consensus:

- trim the institutional-context section by about a third
- shorten the personality-bias paragraph
- soften `deserves attention` to `may complement`
- replace `provided descriptive support` with `was consistent with this pattern`
- remove or reduce some of the more polished phrases in the Discussion
- confirm whether any AI-related acknowledgement/disclosure placement matches journal policy

## What Reviewers Explicitly Do Not Want Changed

This matters as much as the edit list.

The panel does **not** want you to:

- weaken the methodological caveats
- blur the difference between primary and secondary analyses
- make the discordance analysis sound causal
- cut the Korea institutional setting
- make the tone more aggressive
- reframe the science again

## My Synthesis

If I compress the panel into one sentence:

> The manuscript is now journal-fit; it just needs one final prose pass to sound less defended and more finished.

If I compress it into an action list:

1. tighten the Abstract around the baseline joint model
2. trim repeated caution in the Discussion
3. make Results a little more neutral and less interpretive
4. standardize labels
5. clean a few table/text shorthand issues
6. stop there

## Bottom Line

SSM reviewers think the **current version is very close**.

They are not asking you to change what the paper means.

They are asking for a **final editorial polish pass** so that the science you already have reads with a little more confidence, a little less repetition, and a cleaner hierarchy of emphasis.
