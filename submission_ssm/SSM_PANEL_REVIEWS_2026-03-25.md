Note: This is the full synthesized write-up from the simulated 10-editor SSM panel run on the current draft. It is separate from the earlier positioning memo in `manuscript/SSM_EDITOR_PANEL_REVIEWS.md`, which reflected an earlier stage of the project.

## Overall outcome

Panel vote: 9 of 10 editors would send this manuscript for peer review at Social Science & Medicine in its current form.

Minority view: 1 of 10, speaking from a strict editor-in-chief lens, regarded the paper as borderline and thought one more narrative-tightening pass would improve its chances.

Consensus: The manuscript is now reviewable for SSM. The main residual risk is not lack of novelty or scope fit, but small areas of interpretive overreach, especially where baseline, discordance, and time-updated analyses are blended into a single story.

## Review 1: Editor-in-Chief lens

Verdict: Borderline; close, but not an unambiguous send.

This version is much improved and now reads as a plausible SSM submission rather than an epidemiology paper seeking a prestige home. The reframing around economic insecurity in later life, the stronger theoretical anchoring, and the institutional Korea discussion all move the paper in the right direction. The manuscript now has a recognizably social-science identity.

The remaining concern is coherence. The paper still asks the reader to take several analytically distinct things as parts of one overarching conclusion: baseline Cox evidence, discordance evidence, and time-updated evidence. Each is interesting, but together they still feel slightly overintegrated. I would expect careful reviewers to push on whether the manuscript has one clean contribution or several adjacent ones. Relatedly, the comparison between a single subjective appraisal item and more constrained objective indicators remains vulnerable to a measurement-asymmetry critique.

Two additional issues remain visible from a top-level editorial read. First, the mortality outcome is not as cleanly ascertained as one would ideally want because deaths among those lost to follow-up may be missed. Second, the institutional interpretation occasionally leans further than the observed data directly allow. None of that is fatal, but it keeps the paper from feeling like an easy editorial pass.

If I were making the decision alone, I would call this borderline. I could imagine sending it, but I could also imagine declining on the grounds that the story is still slightly more ambitious than the data support.

## Review 2: Social Epidemiology editor

Verdict: Yes, send for review.

This is clearly in scope for the Social Epidemiology office. The question is important, the study population is of wide interest, and the manuscript now does a much better job of explaining why subjective economic dissatisfaction matters as a social exposure rather than treating it simply as a noisy correlate of income. The Korea framing also now works in the right register: it is presented as a useful institutional setting rather than as a culture-bound curiosity.

The strongest features are the large national cohort, the long follow-up, the juxtaposition of subjective and objective economic indicators, and the use of discordance analyses to show that dissatisfaction is not completely reducible to measured material conditions. The theoretical framing is appropriately broad for SSM, and the discussion now sounds like social epidemiology rather than a causal inference methods note.

I would still expect reviewers to focus on three things. First, the manuscript may be pressed on the discrepancy between the baseline joint model and the time-updated joint model. Second, outcome ascertainment through follow-up interviews rather than registry linkage is a real limitation. Third, there is still some unavoidable asymmetry in how the subjective and objective measures are operationalized. Those are reviewer issues, though, not desk-reject issues.

I would send this out.

## Review 3: Quantitative and conceptual rigor editor

Verdict: Yes, reviewable in current form.

The main conceptual improvement is that the manuscript no longer tries to present itself as strong causal epidemiology. That is the correct move. In its current form, the paper is better understood as an associational social epidemiology paper that uses several complementary modeling approaches to illuminate the relationship between economic insecurity and mortality in later life.

The largest remaining coherence issue is interpretive, not technical. The manuscript still occasionally collapses together evidence from baseline joint Cox models, discordance analysis, and time-updated models into a single narrative claim. Those analyses answer related but not identical questions. Reviewers may ask for sharper distinctions between what is established as a between-person baseline association, what is suggested by discordance, and what is observed in the time-updated analyses.

I do not think another analysis is required before review. The methods are already richer than what is necessary to justify peer review. What is required is interpretive discipline, especially around claims that subjective economic dissatisfaction identifies risk that objective indicators "miss" or that it is fully irreducible to income, assets, and welfare participation. That language should stay cautious.

Even with those caveats, the manuscript is reviewable now.

## Review 4: Aging, inequality, and family-structure editor

Verdict: Yes, send for review.

This draft now reads like a meaningful paper about aging, inequality, and economic insecurity, not merely a cohort result from one country. The theoretical framing through stress process and cumulative disadvantage helps, and the institutional discussion connects the empirical findings to broader shifts in welfare provision and family support in later life.

What works especially well is the argument that older adults' economic experience is shaped by more than current income or asset counts. The discordance analysis is useful here because it shows the social experience of economic strain is not fully captured by objective balance-sheet measures. That is a strong aging-and-inequality contribution for SSM readers.

The residual weakness is that the family-support argument remains inferential. The manuscript suggests that weakening intergenerational support changes the meaning of economic insecurity, and that is plausible, but it is not directly demonstrated in these data. I would also be careful not to let the 1997 crisis and Korean specificity dominate the paper's general framing. Those contextual elements help, but they should support the paper, not narrow it.

I would send this for review.

## Review 5: Medical Sociology editor

Verdict: Yes, reviewable.

This is now legitimately legible as a medical sociology-adjacent paper. The most promising move in the revision is the treatment of subjective-objective mismatch as a sociological phenomenon rather than a statistical annoyance. The manuscript now suggests that what matters is not only what resources older adults have, but how economic adequacy is socially appraised in relation to obligations, expectations, and institutional uncertainty.

That is the paper's best route into SSM. In particular, the draft now makes space for the idea that household resources are interpreted within a social field, and that those appraisals may matter for health in ways that are not exhausted by income or wealth. The Korea discussion is also much improved because it emphasizes welfare-state transition and changing family supports rather than resting on essentialized cultural claims.

My only reservation is that the sociological theorization remains disciplined but somewhat modest. There is room to say more about class, kinship, gendered dependence, or stratified vulnerability under partial welfare-state development. But that is an argument for how reviewers may try to deepen the paper, not a reason to keep it from review.

I would send it.

## Review 6: Health Policy editor

Verdict: Yes, send for review.

The manuscript is no longer trying to behave like a policy evaluation, and that is a substantial improvement. It now presents itself as policy-relevant rather than policy-determinative, which is the correct posture given the design. The discussion of Korea's pension limits, out-of-pocket health spending, and changing family support structures gives the reader a credible account of why subjective economic insecurity might matter in this setting.

I especially appreciate the stronger caution around intervention claims. The paper now explicitly says that it does not estimate the effects of specific interventions and that stronger designs would be needed before drawing those conclusions. That sentence materially improves the paper's credibility.

The one place I would still watch is where the manuscript edges toward implying that the institutional features of the Korean setting prove the dissatisfaction signal reflects real structural shortfalls rather than personality-linked negativity. That is suggestive, but still interpretive. Likewise, claims about relevance beyond South Korea should remain careful and conditional.

Even so, this is reviewable now as an observational paper with policy implications.

## Review 7: Medical Anthropology editor

Verdict: Yes, send for review.

The most important improvement is that Korea is now used as a historically and institutionally specific setting rather than as a container for generalized cultural explanation. That shift makes the manuscript far more credible to interdisciplinary readers. The welfare-state transition, incomplete pension protection, and changing family support norms all give the analysis a social world.

I still see small areas where the language could drift toward overgeneralization. In particular, phrasing about filial support changing because younger generations prioritize nuclear family obligations should remain tightly tethered to cited literature rather than sounding like a broad civilizational claim. I would also be careful with any mention of East Asian response styles unless the wording remains clearly limited and methodologically cautious.

Those are manageable issues. The paper's overall trajectory is good, and the anthropological concern I would have had about cultural simplification is no longer dominant. On balance, I would send it for review.

## Review 8: Health Geography editor

Verdict: Yes, send for review.

This is not a geography paper in any narrow sense, but it does use place productively. South Korea functions here as a meaningful institutional context shaped by rapid aging, welfare transition, and historically specific household and family arrangements. That is enough for SSM's broad readership.

The contextual framing could perhaps be strengthened further by a little more attention to historical sequencing and cohort experience, but it already does enough to make the setting analytically useful. I do not see the absence of subnational spatial analysis as a barrier, because the paper is clearly not claiming to answer a geographic distribution question.

The main caution is that when the paper gestures toward broader applicability, it should do so in a way that is explicitly hedged. The Korea context helps make the paper interesting; it should not be flattened into a generic template too quickly. Still, the current version is certainly reviewable.

## Review 9: Health Psychology editor

Verdict: Yes, send for review.

The manuscript is now appropriately restrained in its treatment of subjective appraisal. Earlier versions risked sounding like they were making stronger psychological or mechanistic claims than the data could support. The current draft is better: it treats dissatisfaction as an appraised social experience that may capture dimensions of insecurity not reflected in conventional objective indicators.

That said, there are still a few places where the language edges too close to saying that subjective appraisal itself is an independent mechanism or that objective indicators categorically miss what matters. I would encourage caution there, especially when summarizing the time-updated results and when drawing the concluding implications. The paper is strongest when it says that subjective dissatisfaction is a potentially informative risk marker and weaker when it sounds as though it has isolated a distinct psychological pathway.

Those are wording issues, not reasons to delay review. I would send this out.

## Review 10: Health Economics editor

Verdict: Yes, send for review.

This is not a health economics paper in design, but the institutional and material-security framing is now strong enough that economics-minded readers at SSM would understand why the study matters. The paper does a good job of showing that economic adequacy in later life is not fully captured by a limited set of resource indicators, especially in a setting where public protection is partial and household obligations matter.

I do think the manuscript should remain careful in how it contrasts subjective dissatisfaction with objective measures. Because the measures differ in dimensionality and likely precision, there is a danger in claiming too much about what the objective indicators fail to capture. Likewise, the time-updated findings complicate any simple version of the story in which appraisal is the dominant signal.

That said, the paper now has a coherent enough contribution, a plausible SSM identity, and enough empirical substance to justify peer review. I would send it.

## Cross-cutting themes

Across the 10 reviews, the same themes came up repeatedly:

1. Scope fit is now strong enough for SSM.
2. The theoretical framing is materially better than before and gives the paper a real social-science identity.
3. Korea now functions more as institutional context than as cultural shorthand, which helped substantially.
4. No reviewer thought another analysis was required before peer review.
5. The biggest remaining risk is interpretive overreach, especially where baseline, discordance, and time-updated results are narrated as one seamless story.
6. Measurement asymmetry between the subjective item and the objective indicators remains a likely reviewer critique.
7. Outcome ascertainment and attrition remain limitations reviewers may press on.

## Bottom line

This panel's collective judgment is that the manuscript is now ready to send for peer review at Social Science & Medicine.

The paper is no longer being held back by journal fit or by the lack of a social-science frame. If it struggles in review, the most likely reasons will be overclaim, measure comparability, and limitations of ascertainment rather than the absence of a publishable core contribution.
