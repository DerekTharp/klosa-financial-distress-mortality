Abstract
The Republic of Korea experiences a rapid ageing of population with increased life expectancy and lowered
fertility. National health insurance provides universal access to healthcare for all since 1989, and mandatory
long-term care insurance (LTCI) was introduced in 2008, where everybody who contributes to health
insurance simultaneously contributes to LTCI. Although health services and long-term care are universally
accessible, health differentials remain across socioeconomic groups. LTCI covers about 7% of older people
through eligibility assessment and provides benefits for institutional and home-based care, but cash-benefits
1

in exceptional cases. LTC benefit eligibility has been criticized for being excessively reliant on physical
functionality, and recently eligibility has been extended to people with dementia. In spite of the oversupply of
LTC providers, quality of care has been a concern, which calls for more investment in the quality evaluation
system and training of care workers. There continues to be overreliance on inpatient care and unmet
healthcare needs among LTC users as a result of weak gatekeeping by primary care and a lack of effective
coordination between healthcare and LTC.

I.

Introduction

The republic of Korea (hereafter Korea) has experienced a very rapid ageing of population, and older people
aged 65 and over accounted for 12.7% of the population in 2014 (1). The speed of population ageing in Korea
is projected to be higher than OECD countries, and the old-age dependency ratio, measured by population
aged 65 and over divided by the population aged 15 to 64, will increase from 17.3% (2014) to 71% (2050),
only slightly lower than Spain and Japan (2). The average old-age dependency in OECD countries is 24.8% in
2014, which is projected to increase to 47.3% in 2050 (2).

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

At the same time, family structure and attitudes towards care for older people have changed during the
last decade. The proportion of parents who are living with their children decreased from 38.0% (2008) to
29.2% (2016), with surveys suggesting that a greater share of the Korean population thinks that ‘family,
government and society’ should take care of their parents (45.5%) rather than the share of population citing
‘family’ as the main source of parental support (30.8%) (3). The role of family obligation associated with
caregiving for their parents is expected to decline. Under these circumstances, this paper aims to examine the
health policy responses to ageing in Korea, more specifically health system and long-term care (LTC) system
for older people and lessons for countries of Asia.
This paper is structured as follows. Section II describes key developments in ageing in Korea and the
main characteristics of health system, including spending patterns and population access to health services.
Section III describes LTC system in Korea, including the mandatory long-term care insurance (LTCI) and the
service delivery of LTC as well as coordination issues between healthcare and LTC. Finally, section IV
discusses the main challenges posed by the ageing Korean population to the health and LTC systems, the
policy responses, and potential lessons for other countries in Asia.

II.

Ageing and the Korean Health System

1. Ageing and Health
The life expectancy of Korean people increased from 75.9 to 82.2 during the period of 2000 to 2014 (4). The
leading causes of death among older people were non-communicable diseases (NCDs), like cancer (803.0
persons per 100,000), heart disease (351.0 persons), cerebrovascular disease (311.1 persons), pneumonia
(209.1 persons), diabetes (133.2 persons) in 2015 (Table 1) (5). NCDs, such as low back and neck pain,
ischemic heart disease, lung/liver cancer, diabetes, sense organ diseases, and depressive disorder, were the

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

fast-growing among leading causes of DALYs from 2005 to 2015 (6). Diet was the leading risk factor for
DALYs, followed by smoking, high fasting plasma glucose, alcohol and drug use, high systolic blood
pressure, high body-mass index, and air pollution (6).
From the standpoint of this paper, there are two perspectives related to longevity and health status
that require consideration: “expansion of morbidity” assumes that people live longer with ill health as
longevity increases vulnerability, whereas “compression of morbidity” views life years with illness and
disability decreases as the onset of morbidity is delayed more than life is prolonged (7). In Korea, people with
high socioeconomic status (SES) were more likely to experience the compression of morbidity (8). Good
financial condition was also associated with successful ageing, such as a later life with less diseases and
disease-related disability as well as high level of cognitive and physical functions (9).
On the other hand, lower education was a significant factor explaining physical and mental
difficulties or poor self-rated health (SRH) (10-12), and low levels of income or occupation were predictors
of cognitive impairment (13). In addition, there has been inverse association between socioeconomic factors
and depressive symptoms (14), and nonstandard forms of employments increased the risk of depression in
older population (15). Several studies reported the differential effect of SES on health of older people by
subgroups. For example, women showed faster decline in SRH with age than men, and women’s SRH were
more vulnerable to low-income than men (16). The SES gradients in the burden of diseases showed different
4

distributions by disease groups. The proportion of hypertension, cancer, and diabetes in the burden of diseases
increased with higher education level, but the result was opposite for arthritis or anemia (17).
Although poor health status were highly concentrated among older people with low incomes, strong pro-rich
inequality exists for healthcare expenditure (18). Inequality in inpatient or outpatient service utilization has
remained even after adjusting for health status among older adults (19). A literature review showed that
income, wealth, education, previous or current employment status, housing, and region were predictors of
health expenditure of older people (20). Under the relatively weak Korean social pension system, low income

elderly faced considerable financial burden due to health expenditure, resulting in lower healthcare service
use (21). However, an income-related horizontal equity index showed negative or zero values for home-based
LTC utilization (22), implying an equitable use of home care services under public LTCI, unlike the pro-rich
inequality of healthcare utilization.
In summary, although Korea has experienced an increase in life expectancy, there are still disparities
in health and functional status by SES groups. Moreover, there are inequality issues for healthcare utilization
and expenditure under the national health insurance (NHI) due to high out-of-pocket (OOP) payment at the
point of services, although the gaps have been reduced in LTCI.
2. Healthcare Access and Health Expenditure
Korean health system with mandatory NHI has provided universal access to healthcare for all people
regardless of age. It achieved universal coverage of population in 1989 and has operated a single insurer
scheme with a uniform contribution schedule and benefits coverage since 2000 (23).
With population ageing, NHI needs to extend the benefit package to cover NCDs and services for
older people. For example, the major NCD groups, like circulatory, digestive, muscular, cancer, endocrine
and mental health, account for 60% of health spending in Korea (24). Although several programs have been
implemented for NCD management (such as cancer screening, extension of coverage for NCD medicines, and

5

pilots for community primary care), there are still many uncovered (uninsured) services, resulting in high
OOP payment (25). Moreover, as a large portion of older population suffer from poverty (e.g., the rate of
relative poverty among older people was 49.6%, the highest in the OECD) (26), a contributory social
insurance without sufficient subsidy to the poor (only 3% of population is covered by a tax-funded Medical
Aid Program) still faces challenges related to healthcare access.
The share of GDP allocated to health spending (excluding spending on investments) in Korea was
7.2% in 2015, compared with an OECD average of 8.9% (27). Per capita health spending has been growing

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

continuously and has outpaced the average per capita spending in OECD (27) although the growth has slowed
since 2011 (28). In 2015, public sources, i.e., social health insurance and taxes, accounted for only 55.6% of
overall health spending, well below the OECD average of 72.8% (27). Health expenditure of older people,
which was 12.3% of total population, accounted for 37.8% of total health spending in 2015 (Table 2) (29).
Older people’s health spending is driven by quantity of healthcare utilization (e.g., number of inpatient days,
outpatient visits) rather than per-day expenditure (29).
Although Korea has introduced several programs for responding to NCDs and ageing, there has been
an over-reliance on hospital care with weak gatekeeping by primary care and referral systems. The
fragmentation and the lack of coordination in healthcare is a big threat to the efficiency and quality of care for
older people, who have multi-morbidities. The average annual growth rate of health expenditure of total
population in inpatient care was higher (12.4%) than outpatient care (8.2%) or retail pharmaceuticals (7.2%)
from 2004 to 2015 (Table 3) (30).
Proximity of death also has a crucial impact on health expenditure of a life course. The death-related
(one year before death) expense was more than 9 times higher than survivors, but the amount of expense
declined with age (31). Among the death-related health expenditure, the share of injection-related cost,
hospitalization cost, and examination cost accounted for about 60% of total costs (31), implying that many
patients spend their last part of life in hospitals with injections or over-examinations.
6

Lengthy hospitalization (often social admissions that do not really need medical care) due to limited
coordination with LTC, and clinical decision on treatment such as intensity of care at the end stage of life are
also key determinants of expenditure because the cost of hospital stay is much higher than that of LTC
institutions or hospice care. On the other hand, it was reported that per capita spending on retail
pharmaceuticals reached a peak at the age of 70--74 rather than 75 and older (24). Survival of the healthy
elderly or high burden of OOP payment might lead those old-old population (75 and older) to reduce their
consumption of retail pharmaceuticals and related expenditure (24).

III.

Long-term Care System in Korea

1. Long-term Care Insurance
In July 2008, Korea introduced public LTCI, in response to rapid population ageing and low income security
for older people (32). Besides demographic changes, family structures also contributed to the growing
demands for LTC. As female labor participation increased, family size became smaller, and the attitude to
care for older people (regarding family obligation) changed, the availability of informal caregiver has been
diminished (32).
There were some debates on whether Korea, whose proportion of older people was still lower than in
OECD countries, should introduce a universal LTCI rather than a public assistance-type LTC support for poor
older people. Progressive government played a key role in the introduction of universal LTCI to cover all
older people who have need for LTC. Kim Dae Jung, who became president in early 1998, had a strong
interest in progressive welfare state policies. After becoming president, Kim initiated several expansive
reforms in the health and social policy arenas, such as unemployment insurance, national pension, NHI, and
minimum living standard guarantee (MLSG) (33). Formal discussion of LTCI started in 2000 when the
Ministry of Health and Welfare appointed a Planning Committee for Elderly Long-Term Care. The provision
of LTC aiming to expand care for older people was easy to embrace because it had broad appeal to the public
and did not raise political opposition (34). President Kim Dae Jung was succeeded by Roh Moo-hyun in
7

January, 2003, who was from the same political party as Kim and included a promise to enact LTCI in his
presidential campaign. After more than one year of consideration, public hearings, and research on other
countries, government decided to introduce social insurance for LTC with a tax subsidy.
The funding of the new LTCI is separate from the NHI although both are administered by National
Health Insurance Service (NHIS) to reduce the administrative costs. Different from European countries (e.g.,
Germany) where LTC system has been developed for people with disability including older people, LTCI of
Korea was introduced in the context of population ageing. As a result, people aged 65 years or older are

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

eligible for all types of LTC, but eligibility of those under 65 is restricted to aged-related LTC need, such as
individuals with geriatric diseases (i.e. dementia, cerebrovascular disease). The design of LTCI that younger
people pay contribution but their eligibility for benefits is restricted has resulted in a big inter-generational
transfer and contributed to the financial sustainability of LTCI.
The contribution rate is 6.55% of health insurance premium, in other words, anyone who pays health
insurance contribution pays LTCI contribution too. The financing mix is composed of contributions (6065%), tax subsidies (20%), and copayment by service users, which is 20% for institutional service and 15%
for home-based service. The coinsurance rate for institutional care is higher than that for home-based care in
order to promote de-institutionalization and community-based care, and there is 50% subsidy for copayment
to the low-income group (17.1% of LTC users in June 2014) and zero copayment for the beneficiaries of
Medical Aid program (35). The average monthly OOP payment (for covered and uncovered services) was
477,000 KRW (about US$ 430) for institutional care and 110,000 KRW (about US$ 100) for home-based
care in 2014 (35).
To use LTC services, individuals have to pass need assessment, which assesses functional status in
physical, cognitive, behavior, nursing care, and rehabilitative characteristics using 52 items. The needs
assessment for eligibility of LTCI is a major difference from health insurance. The eligible group was
classified into three levels, which were extended to five levels for those with dementia (which are designated
8

as level 5) since July 2014 (36). Before the revision, if a person had cognitive disorders but without severe
physical limitations, he or she was not eligible to LTCI. Therefore lots of people with dementia, both patients
and family members, suffered from the lack of adequate LTC.
The population coverage of LTCI increased from 2.9% to 7.2% of older people from July 2008 to
June 2016 (Figure 1) (37). The utilization rate has increased from 53% to 91% (of those who eligible) at the
same periods, as awareness of LTCI system has improved. However, the increasing number of certified
individuals and service users have caused a concern on sustainability issues. The need assessment became

stricter for the eligibility levels 1 or 2 (severe condition), leading to the decline in the proportion of levels 1 or
2 from 42% (April 2010) to 23% (June 2016) (Figure 2) (37).
LTCI provides benefits for institutional and home-based care, and cash benefits are available only in
exceptional cases, e.g., when no service providers are accessible in the region. The amount of benefits
depends on the eligibility level, and the ceiling on benefit coverage is different by the level. (In contrast, there
is no ceiling on benefits but a ceiling on OOP payment in the NHI.) For example, the maximum monthly
benefits range from 784,100 KRW (about US$ 660) for level 5 to 1,196,900 KRW (about US$ 1,000) for
level 1 in the case of home-based care (38). The individuals with less severe level, e.g., levels 3 to 5, are
recommended to use home-based care to avoid over-reliance on institutional care. The payment type varies by
service types, such as pay per visit (service hours) for home-visit care and pay per day for institutional care.
The proportion of institutional care among the total expenditure for LTCI increased from 43.3% to
51.3%, while home-based care decreased from 56.7% to 48.7% between 2009 and 2015. The home-based
care is composed of home-visit care, home-visit bathing, home-visit nursing, day and night care, shot-term
care, and welfare equipment. Within the home-based care, more than 70% of benefit has been spent for
“home-visit care”, but only 0.5% for “home-visit nursing” (Table 4) (37).

2. Long-term Care Service Delivery
After the introduction of LTCI, the number of LTC providers rapidly expanded, from 2,600 to 5,000
institutions and 11,900 to 12,900 home-based care agencies since 2009 (37). It also created diverse jobs
related with LTC but led to the oversupply of service providers. The number of care workers and nurse aides
increased dramatically as they need shorter period of education and training than registered nurses or doctors.
For example, the number of care workers rapidly increased from 176,500 to 301,700 and nurse aides from
4,200 to 9,770, but that of registered nurses changed from 3,400 to 2,800 (Table 5) (37).

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

The increase in quasi-professional staffing may be caused by small-sized institutions, e.g., capacity of
less than 30 residents or group homes (less than 10 residents), where the entry to market is relatively easy for
private sector providers (39). The rapid increase in the supply of providers was not accompanied by quality of
care and equitable distribution by regions. About 70--80% of providers are from the private sector, and the
majority of home care providers are concentrated in urban areas (39).
To assure the quality of care in the LTC sector, NHIS has implemented quality evaluation system
since 2009. The number of quality indicators varies by types of service providers, e.g., 88 items for
institutional care and 32--59 items for home-based care, and they are grouped by five domains of quality
measurement, namely, management of institutions, environment and safety, rights and responsibilities,
process of services, and outcome of services (40). The result of evaluation score (A-E) has been publicly
disseminated through an official LTCI website, and the high performance institutions have received
incentives of 1--2% additional reimbursement (40). Although the LTC quality evaluation system was initiated
to improve the quality of care, there are still several limitations. The gap between real quality of care and
evaluation results exists due to the insufficient number of outcome-based indicators. Inadequate cooperation
between local governments and NHIS has been criticized because local governments are not active in
controlling the quality of LTC institutions even though they have the authority to approve or close the
operation of LTC institutions (41).

3. Coordination of Health and Long-term Care
Although the utilization of LTCI have effects on reducing the length of stay in acute hospitals among the
older people with severe functional limitations (42) or on decreasing the costs of LTC hospitals (43), policy
challenges remain associated with the lack of coordination between healthcare and LTC. For example,
overlapped inpatient services are provided by LTC institutions (which is covered by LTCI) and LTC hospitals
(which is covered by NHI) for older people with similar health and functional status. (LTC (or geriatric)
hospitals are required to have physicians whereas the maximum requirement for medical personnel in LTC

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

institutions is nurse aide.) With the lack of effective coordination between LTC institutions and LTC
hospitals, they were often regarded as substitutes for each other. This resulted in persistent social admissions
in LTC hospitals among older people with lower medical care needs (44). On the other hand, a significant
portion of older people with clinical care needs stay in LTC institutions where healthcare service is not
provided (45). Some older people even without the need for medical treatment want to stay in LTC hospitals
as they are worried that a referral to hospitals is not well arranged by LTC institutions.
The coordination failure between health insurance and LTCI has to do with the history and path
dependency in the development of LTC system for older people in Korea. With population ageing and
increased need for LTC of older people, government introduced LTC hospitals, reimbursed by NHI. Before
the LTCI was introduced, many older people in LTC hospitals were reluctant to be discharged to LTC
institutions because they had to pay for LTC institutions while the majority of the cost of LTC hospitals were
funded by NHI. As a result, social admissions were prevalent in LTC hospitals, which called for the need for
public funding such as LTCI for LTC institutions.
There are several institutional factors contributing to the persistent social admissions in LTC hospitals.
The majority of LTC hospitals and LTC institutions are private, and more patients/residents mean profits for
them. They have mushroomed over the last ten years, and competition has been fierce. Benefits package of
NHI tends to be more generous than LTCI, e.g., ceiling on OOP pay for every six months in NHI. As a result,
11

after a few months of stay in LTC hospitals, OOP payments can be lower than that in LTC institutions. There
is a financial penalty, i.e., reduced fees, for LTC hospitals when patients stay for more than 6 months. But
lower fee (for a given copayment rate) means lower cost sharing or OOP pay for patients, resulting in
financial incentive for longer length of stay in LTC hospitals.
IV.

Discussions and Policy Lessons

Korea has experienced an increase in life expectancy and improved health status among older population
thanks to universal access to healthcare for all under NHI system and public LTCI. Although the universal

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

access to health and LTC services has been achieved, there are still health disparities and healthcare gaps by
SES groups. Moreover, even though several programs have been implemented in response to the increasing
burden of NCDs and an ageing population, there is still an over-reliance on hospital care with weak
gatekeeping, leading to fragmentation and low quality. The lack of coordination between healthcare and LTC
impeded the original goal of LTCI, therefore there are still many social admissions in hospitals, meanwhile,
many of older people with clinical care needs are staying in LTC institutions without adequate healthcare
service. Insufficient training for health professionals for older population also contributes to low-quality care,
even if hospitals and LTC providers are being over-supplied.
At the same time, concerns have been raised about the sustainability of health and LTC system under
the rapidly growing demand and expenditure for geriatric and personal care (46, 47). The reduction in social
admissions and strengthening policies for prevention and health promotion for older people might directly or
potentially bring financial savings (48).
There is also a tradeoff between population coverage and financial sustainability of the LTC system.
A strict assessment for eligibility can improve the financial sustainability, but the resultant low coverage of
population can limit the capacity of the LTC system to meet the LTC needs of older people. The current
Korean LTCI covers about 7% of older people, which is significantly lower than for OECD countries
(average is more than 10%) (49). The LTCI eligibility has recently been extended for people with dementia
12

(36) because the original criteria could not fully consider the hardship of caring for older adults with
cognitive or psychological conditions. This experience showed the importance of considering the unmet
needs of older population when a government set a need assessment scheme for LTC eligibility.
Among home-based care, more than 70% of benefits have been concentrated on “home-visit care”,
and there is an urgent need to encourage the utilization of diversified types of home and community-based
services. The development of service programs in day-care centers and facilitating the role of home-visit
nursing, in collaboration with primary care doctors, would be needed. If the home-based care services can be

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

linked with community-based primary care (e.g. helping medication adherence or monitoring of daily health)
(50), the health and functional status of older people would be improved while expensive acute
hospitalizations or institutional care can be reduced.
Changing role of families and societal perceptions regarding filial piety or care for older people also
should not be overlooked. The recent changes in household structures and attitude to caregiving might have
an effect on social admissions or LTC utilization. For some older adults, longer length of stay in hospitals or
depending on formal LTC services are inevitable when they do not have family members to rely on for
caregiving.
The experience of Korea provides many other policy lessons for countries in Asia. Primary care is an
essential component of universal health coverage (UHC) and becomes even more important in an era of
population ageing. Limited role of primary care system has contributed to the inefficient health system and
the lack of coordination between health system and LTC system in Korea. Primary care provider needs to
play a key role in prevention and promotion of health and to overcome the inefficiencies of hospital-centric
care. Cost-effective continuum of care requests training for primary care doctors to enforce their capacity of
team work with LTC service providers (51) as well as multi-occupational collaborative training programs
(including health and LTC service providers and administrative staffs) to facilitate their effective role sharing,
as evidenced in Japan (52). Local centers can be another option, as a unified access point to individuals with
13

diverse needs, encompassing community-based healthcare, home-visit nursing station, and social welfare
council (48, 51). In addition, several forms of discharge services (e.g. discharge planning, discharge
summary, and discharge conference) might be a useful tool for supporting the coordination among acute care
and primary care or LTC (53-55).
Policy choice between universal or targeted LTC system is an issue, too. In most low- and middleincome countries (LMICs), governments still have policy priority on UHC than on universal access to LTC.
Considering the speed of ageing and fiscal space, government can start with LTC targeting poor older people

Downloaded by [189.60.120.16] at 03:02 04 August 2017

or designing cost sharing depending on the capacity to pay. In Korea, subsidy and reduction in copayment to
the low-income group facilitated them to use LTC services (56). LTC has the characteristics of convenience
and quality of life, instead of life or death, and governments of LMICs still need to balance the return on
investment for different population groups, such as children and older people. Social consensus and the
changing role of family/informal care, as well as the evidence on the relation between healthcare and LTC for
older people, e.g., whether LTC saves healthcare cost by reducing the need for healthcare, should be
considered.
