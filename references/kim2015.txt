Abstract
This study was conducted to investigate the prevalence of unmet need based on both subjective
assessment and adjustments for need-predicted utilization and to examine factors associated
with them. Data from the Fourth Korea National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey, a
population-based, cross-sectional study, were used in this study. Participants included 11 620
Koreans (4959 males and 6661 females) aged 19 and older. The results for subjective unmet
need suggested that female or younger individuals were more likely to experience unmet need,
compared to male or older individuals. Those residing outside the capital had a higher likelihood
of experiencing unmet need. Lower levels of education showed an association with higher odds
of having unmet need. This study found similar results for the adjusted unmet need. The findings
of this study suggest that location and education are more important factors associated with
unmet need than income levels.
Keywords
health equity, population health, heath economics and financing, health care services

Introduction
According to the World Health Report 2000, published by the World Health Organization, ensuring access to health care based on need, rather than ability to pay, should be an important means
to improving health.1 Like most health care systems in high-income countries, Korea also strives
to achieve equity goals in terms of ensuring access to needed health care regardless of socioeconomic status. Particularly, it has a national health insurance system, funded both publicly and
privately, under which every citizen is covered for health care and medical treatment; because
there are no restrictions with regard to which health care providers patients can use, citizens can
visit virtually any private physician or health care facility.2 However, there is still room for
improvement in the Korean health care system, because there are financial and other barriers,
such as the limitation of benefit coverage, as well as high out-of-pocket payments, that patients
face in accessing care. This may have resulted in inequity and differential utilization of medical
care between subgroups of individuals and thereby inequitable distribution of services.3

Assessment of the extent to which individuals are able to access necessary health services
based on medical need, rather than other factors, can provide evidence as to whether the health
care system should develop policies for redistribution of health care resources in order to address
the issue.4 One widely used method for measuring inequity is to determine whether factors other
than need for health care affect health care utilization.5,6 Inequity then arises, for example, when
individuals in higher socioeconomic groups are more likely to use or are using a greater quantity
of health services than those in lower socioeconomic groups, after controlling for their level of
ill health.
This study had the following objectives. It used the Korea National Health and Nutrition
Examination Survey (KNHANES) Quaternary materials to identify unmet need experience
among adults and to examine factors associated with the experience. In addition, it also examined
how factors associated with unmet need differ using 2 approaches, that is, subjective unmet need
and adjusted unmet need.

Background on Unmet Need
In this study, 2 approaches were used for measurement of “unmet need,” or any need for health
care that remains because appropriate health care was not received. One approach, “subjective
unmet need,” relies on individuals’ subjective assessments that they have not received the care
that they need. It has been very feasible because numerous existing surveys include questions
pertaining to unmet need; in some ways, it is also superior, since, arguably, individuals are better
able to estimate their health status7 and are in a unique position to identify shortcomings in their
experiences with health care. Subjective assessments of unmet need may also include information on the reasons for the unmet need, which can then be used to focus policy actions. The other
approach, “adjusted unmet need,” compares estimated utilization of health care services based on
the main determinants of seeking care and actual utilization, and the difference between those 2
factors is regarded as unmet need. The reason for treating subjective unmet need as a nonneed
variable, even though it explicitly refers to “need,” warrants an explanation: consistent with the
conventional method of measuring income-related inequity, we defined need as health, age, and
sex, the main determinants of seeking care in the Aday and Andersen terminology.5,6 Any perception of interaction between the health care system and the individual, even the so-called unmet
need is, in that conventional framework, considered as a nonneed variable; it is not an upstream
determinant of care seeking but rather a variable that explains variations in utilization for a given
level of health.
Prior research on subjective unmet need has focused on measurement of its prevalence and
investigation of its individual- and system-level predictors. Most studies have presumed that
subjective unmet need represents barriers to access, and indeed, many survey questions are
phrased to measure that subjective unmet need that arises through barriers to access, such as cost.
Specifically, previous studies of unmet need conducted in the United States suggested a strong
relationship between unmet need and lower income and/or being uninsured or underinsured.8-10
However, in countries with universal health care coverage, such as Europe and Canada, research
on unmet need has been less extensive than in the United States, perhaps because of the relative
lack of direct cost-based barriers to care. Studies conducted in Canada have identified some
population groups with increased likelihood of reporting an unmet health care need, such as
women, people in worse health, nonelderly, higher educated, lower income, nonimmigrants,
urban residents, and individuals without prescription drug insurance.11-15
Studies of unmet need in Korea have focused either on the general population or subgroups
based on age, income level, and certain diseases. Song et al reported that female or older individuals were less likely than male or younger ones to have unmet needs. They also reported that those
without private supplemental health insurance showed an association with higher likelihood of

and diabetes and the level of unmet need,21 or male adults aged 50 or older with mild lower urinary tract symptoms and benign prostatic hyperplasia,22 or female workers specifically.23
Based on the above, this study attempted to provide empirical evidence on unmet need by
employing a comprehensive set of variables and investigated the association between subjective
unmet need and conventional methods of measuring unmet need based on need-adjusted utilization by examining the difference between actual and need-predicted utilization.

Methods
Data Source and Study Population
The KNHANES is conducted by the Korea Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. The
purpose of KNHANES is to provide representative and reliable statistical data on the health
level, health-related attitudes and behavior, and dietary and nutrition status of Koreans. Four
surveys have been completed thus far: I (1998), II (2001), III (2005), and IV (2007-2009). The
KNHANES IV is composed of a health survey (health interview and health consciousness behavior), an examination survey, and a nutrition examination survey. Among them, this study used
raw data from a health interview survey.
Our analysis compared the difference between subjective unmet need and adjusted unmet
need by modeling the probability and then intensity of use on a comprehensive set of needrelated and socioeconomic factors. To obtain the need-predicted utilization, the nonneed (and
subjective unmet need) variables were held constant at their sample means during the prediction
stage.24,25 For the subjective unmet need, we attempted to determine whether people with different characteristics are more likely to have a probability of unmet need; for the adjusted unmet
need, the difference between actual and need-adjusted utilization (the residual, or unexplained,
utilization) is modeled on a set of socioeconomic variables.

Dependent Variables
Subjective unmet need was measured by the following question: “During the past 12 months,
was there ever a time when you felt you needed health care but you didn’t receive it?” Health care
utilizations were divided into outpatient and inpatient visits. Outpatient utilization was measured
by the following question: “During the past 2 weeks, was there ever a time when you received
outpatient services without being admitted to hospitals?” Respondents were provided with the
frequencies of and “yes” or “no” for outpatient service utilization.
Inpatient utilization was measured by the following question: “During the past 12 months,
was there ever a time when you were hospitalized?” Respondents were provided with the frequencies of and “yes” or “no” for inpatient service utilization.

This study also examined the difference between the actual utilization and need-predicted
utilization, which is the result of regression analysis after controlling for sex, age, and diseases.

Independent Variables
A comprehensive set of demographic, socioeconomic, enabling, and health-related factors were
used for estimation of need for health care. Demographic factors included age measured in 6
categories (19-29, 30-39, 40-49, 50-59, 60-69, and 70 plus), sex, marital status (married, widowed, not married), and residential areas. Socioeconomic factors that have been shown to influence health care utilization were included in the models. These included the highest level of
education attained (less than elementary, middle school graduate, high school graduate, or college graduate or higher), whether the individual was participating in economic activities
(employed or unemployed). Enabling factors included adjusted household income (deciles),
number of people within the household, public health insurance type, and having private supplemental health insurance plans. Adjusted household income incorporates the fact that individual
utility may differ according to the number of people within the household. Health-related factors
included self-assessed health based on 5 categories (excellent, very good, good, fair, and poor),
a dummy variable that equals 1 if the individual reported fair or poor self-assessed mental health
(0 if good, very good, or excellent), reported chronic conditions (none, 1, 2, or more), having
cancer, and whether the individual reported no, moderate, or severe activity limitations due to
health, and a dummy to indicate smokes daily and drinks heavily (5 or more drinks) at least once
per week.

Statistical Analyses
KNHANES was based on a complex sample design. Therefore, all statistical analyses were performed using the survey procedure of SAS version 9.2 (SAS Inc, Cary, NC) specifically designed
for analysis of such sample survey results. In the survey procedure, information pertaining to
complex sample designs, such as stratification, clustering, and unequal weighting, was combined
for estimation of the parameters.
The annual unmet need prevalence rate was calculated for the entire adult population and
compared between men and women. Logistic regression analyses were performed to test the
statistical significance of various factors on the likelihood of experiencing unmet need. To determine whether there were statistically significant differences in various factors between the unmet
need and non-unmet need populations, a t test was used for continuous variables; the Rao–Scott
χ2 test (design-adjusted χ2 test) was used for categorical variables. For all statistical analyses, the
results were considered statistically significant when P < .05.

Results
Characteristics varied across individuals who did and did not report subjective unmet need (see
Table 1). Female subjects have a higher rate of unmet need (27.4%) than male subjects (18.8%).
Subjects aged 70 and older report the highest rate of unmet need (25.2%) than those in any other
age group. In addition, residential areas and education level differed across those with unmet
need experience and those without. Individuals living in town or country and other areas had a
higher rate of unmet need (25.0% and 23.3%) than those living in metropolis (20.9%). Those
with a lower level of education were likely to report a higher rate of unmet need; in particular,
those with elementary school education or below showed the highest rate of unmet need (27.4%).

With this, the rate of unmet need based on utilization was 12.7% for outpatient services and 6.4%
for inpatient services. Descriptive statistics related to adjusted unmet need are shown in Table 3. For the
adjusted unmet need of outpatient care, women reported a higher rate of unmet need (18.7%) than men
(6.6%). Residential areas were not statistically different for adjusted unmet need for outpatient care
across different areas. However, for the adjusted unmet need for inpatient care, those living in the capital city had a statistically significant lower rate (5.0%), followed by metropolitan areas (5.8%) and other
areas (7.2%; P < .0001). According to education level, those with lower levels of education appeared to
have a higher rate of unmet need for both outpatient and inpatient care than those with higher levels of
education (P < .0001). Employed people had a slightly higher rate of unmet need (13.5% and 7.1%)
than the unemployed (11.9% and 5.7%) for both outpatient and inpatient care. Household income level
was not statistically different in unmet need for outpatient care. Health condition, however, appeared to
be a critical factor in adjusted unmet need. Adjusted unmet need for outpatient care based on the subjective health condition showed an inverse-U-shaped curve: very good (9.3%), good (12.9%), fair (14.3%),
bad (11.7%), and very bad (6.3%; P < .0001). Prevalence of chronic disease showed an association with
different percentage of adjusted unmet need for both outpatient and inpatient care: those with 1 condition had the highest percentage of adjusted unmet need for outpatient care (14.8%), followed by no
chronic condition (12.3%) and 2 or more (11.7%; P = .0033). Similarly, those with 1 condition (7.5%)
had the highest percentage of unmet need for inpatient care (7.2%), followed by no chronic condition
(6.8%) and 2 or more (1.2%; P < .0001). Those with cancer were found to have higher rates of unmet
need for outpatient care (20.6%), compared to those who did not have cancer (12.6%; P = .0043).
Health behaviors did not show statistically significant results.
Finally, the results of multivariate logistic regression on unmet need after adjusting for needpredicted utilization based on age, sex, and health condition are shown in Table 4. For outpatient
care, those residing outside the capital were less likely to experience unmet need: odds ratios for
metropolitan areas and other areas were 0.640 (95% CI = 0.520-0.788) and 0.651 (95% CI =
0.540-0.785), respectively. The influence of education level showed strong significance.
Compared to individuals who graduated college or higher, those who had a lower than college
graduate level of education were much more likely to experience unmet need. Specifically, the
odds ratios were 21.303 (95% CI = 16.405-27.664) for elementary school graduate or less, 8.365
(95% CI = 6.407-10.922) for middle school graduate, and 1.832 (95% CI = 1.418-2.366) for high
school graduate. Among socioeconomic factors, compared to those with private supplemental
health insurance, those without were 18.4% more likely to have unmet need for outpatient care
(OR = 1.184, 95% CI = 1.020-1.375).

described above. After adjusting for need-predicted utilization, those with lower levels of education were more likely to experience unmet need for inpatient care. Compared to individuals who
graduated college or higher, those who had a lower than college graduate level of education were
much more likely to experience unmet need. Specifically, the odds ratios were 38.629 (95% CI =

adjusted unmet need for outpatient care. This may suggest that individuals living in areas other
than the capital city perceived that they had experienced unmet need for health care; however,
when adjusting for need-predicted utilization based on age, sex, and health condition, they
were, in fact, less likely to have unmet need, particularly for outpatient care. Although we did
not attempt to determine the exact reasons for the different influences of residential areas on
subjective versus adjusted unmet need, we suspect that those living in areas other than the capital city may feel that they are somewhat disadvantaged in terms of access to health care because
a large number of major hospitals and leading health care providers are located in the capital
city. However, the results for adjusted unmet need showed the opposite case: after adjusting for
need-predicted utilization, those living in the capital city were actually more likely to have
unmet need for outpatient care.
Second, the finding that the odds ratios for the lower level of education became much larger
after adjusting for need-predicated utilization suggested that education level played the greatest
role in influencing unmet need for health care, except for age, sex, and health condition. Although
we did not assess the association between unmet need and health outcome, we can reasonably
assume that a higher rate of unmet need may be related to negative health outcome among those
with a lower level of education. A prior study showed that individuals with good subjectively
rated health and 1 or no diseases had a higher level of education.26 In addition, previous studies
in Korean subjects have reported differences in mortality based on educational level.27,28 This
suggests that education is an important determinant for both access to care and health outcome in
Korea.
Although not as important as residential areas and education, results for other variables are
worth mentioning. Unlike previous studies,19,20 this study did not find significant results for
income level in unmet need. However, because not having private health insurance is associated
with a higher likelihood of adjusted unmet need for outpatient care, one’s ability to pay for the
purchase of supplemental coverage still appears to exert some effects on access to health care.
A recent publication suggested the need for action to address the structural drivers and conditions of daily living that affect health inequities in the Asia Pacific context.29 While there are
signs of action and hope, substantial challenges remain for health equity in Asia Pacific. This
study adds to the current body of literature by showing that residential areas and education are

greater barriers to health care access than cost barriers due to differences in income in Korea.
This study will be helpful to policy makers in formulation of strategies to lessen the discrepancies
in utilization of health care among individuals with different characteristics.
This study was not without limitations. First, information on monthly income and health care
utilization relied on the subjective response in the Fourth KNHANES. In particular, in the case
of acute disease, the recall period addressed was 2 weeks; thus, there may be significant seasonal
variation in acute disease. However, we were unable to adjust for the potential issue stemming
from the recall period. Second, this study assumed that need factors for health care utilization
were limited to age, sex, and diseases. Some previous studies used not only these 3 variables but
also subjective health status, number of chronic conditions, and limited daily activities; therefore,
the results might have been affected accordingly. Third, in the indirect method of estimating
unmet need using a regression model, we acknowledge that different strata of society generally
vary in their general level of health care use: in general, poor people may use lower levels of
health care services; therefore, their mean use will differ from a different stratum, whose age and
sex composition may be different. Because we used the difference between 2 overall means—the
calculated and the actual—to represent the gap, this confounder may have been left unaccounted
for. Finally, this study employed a cross-sectional design, which does not permit inference of
causal relationships; therefore, caution is needed while interpreting the study results.
The results of our study suggested the presence of inequity in health care access in Korea,
since individuals in higher education groups are more likely to use health services than those in
lower education groups, after controlling for age, sex, and their level of health condition. In addition, the finding expands on previous literature by adding global evidence for the association of
nonneed factors with health care access in Asia and provides evidence of the need for development of policies within the Korean health care system in order to address the issue.
