Experiencing a low socioeconomic status (SES) throughout the life course has been reported to be
correlated with poor health outcomes. Several studies have suggested that income, wealth, and perceptions of economic status are associated with increased risk of death among elderly people. Few
studies have investigated the association between lifetime SES and mortality among elderly adults. The
analysis in this study was based on 2310 elderly adults for whom SES data from the four phases of the
longitudinal survey of Health and Living Status of the Elderly in Taiwan (1989, 1993, 1996, and 1999) were
available, and who were alive in 1999. The SES measures included in the analysis were annual income,
the household wealth, and the self-perceived economic satisfaction. A group-based trajectory modelling
approach was employed to create SES trajectories. Cox proportional hazard models were employed to
examine the association between SES trajectories and 8-year all-cause mortality (1999e2007). Irrespective of whether income, wealth, or self-perceived economic satisfaction was used, the elderly adults
with consistently low SES trajectory throughout early old age were independently and signiﬁcantly
associated with higher hazards of mortality than were those in a consistently high SES trajectory.
Downward or upward mobility of income and wealth were associated with increased hazard of mortality.
However, decreased self-perceived economic satisfaction was not signiﬁcantly associated with increased
hazard of mortality. According to the results, the strong distinction between trajectory patterns of income, wealth, and self-perceived economic satisfaction among elderly adults indicate that neither should
be overlooked when investigating the role of SES mobility in mortality. Retirement policies or strategies
for maintaining and promoting favorable SES in early old age may beneﬁt the health of elderly adults
later in life.

1. Introduction
Experiencing a low socioeconomic status (SES) throughout the
life course has been reported to be correlated with poor health
outcomes (Ben-Shlomo and Kuh, 2002); examples include takinh
sick leave from work during adulthood (Lallukka et al., 2013), cardiovascular disease mortality during adulthood (Johnson-Lawrence
et al., 2013), and physiological function (Gruenewald et al., 2012).
There is a growing interest in how variations or pattern changes in
SES throughout life inﬂuence the health of elderly adults (Davey

Smith et al., 1997; Hallqvist et al., 2004; Næss et al., 2006). However, evidence of the relationships between SES trajectories and
health in old age is scarce, and only two studies have been widely
acknowledged (Næss et al., 2006; Nicholson et al., 2005). Because
the populations in numerous societies are aging rapidly, the health
and welfare of elderly adults, particularly those with a low SES,
have become a major focus of policymakers. The relationship between SES trajectories and health in early old age may be a crucial
empirical reference for policymakers planning retirement policies
or welfare programs targeting the elderly population.
A life course approach to socioeconomic inequalities in health
proposes several hypotheses on how socially patterned exposures
to advantages or adversity throughout the life course inﬂuence late
health outcomes (Ben-Shlomo and Kuh, 2002; Davey Smith et al.,

1997; Hallqvist et al., 2004). The proposed mechanisms can be
summarized in four hypotheses. First, the critical period model
hypothesizes that exposures to advantages or adversity during a
speciﬁc period has lasting or lifelong effects on subsequent health.
Second, because adversity and advantages may lead to one another,
the chain of risk model describes how a sequence of linked exposures increases risks of morbidity and mortality. Third, the risk
accumulation model suggests that different types of exposures
gradually accumulate throughout the life course, increasing the
risks of morbidity and mortality. Fourth, the social mobility model
suggests that in contemporary society, several types of social
mobility such as downward or upward social mobility are crucial to
late health and mortality. The risk accumulation model could
explain the doseeresponse relationship between cumulated
quantities of social adversity and mortality, whereas the social
mobility model takes into account the temporal sequence of
adversity inﬂuences. That is, analysis of social trajectory allows to
identifying, in detail, stages in the life course that have a detrimental impact. Social trajectory analysis incorporating SES at three
or more time periods provides informative trajectories to understand SES processes or pathways of mortality inequality, particularly at old age.
Although SES is a multidimensional social and economic
concept (Elo, 2009; Mackenbach, 2012), numerous researchers
have used only a single SES indicator or a single dimension of indicators when analyzing the inﬂuence SES exerts on health inequalities (Pollitt et al., 2005). Commonly used indicators include
income and wealth. However, in addition to these measures,
increasing studies have emphasized the psychosocial effects of SES
on health outcome (Nobles et al., 2013). In other words, the sense of
economic adequacy or a person's perception of his or her SES might
play a crucial role in mediating or moderating the relationship
between material resources and health outcome (Demakakos et al.,
2008; Nobles et al., 2013; Schnittker and McLeod, 2005). Relevant
measures include the social status ladder (Adler et al., 2008;
Demakakos et al., 2008; Hu et al., 2005; Kopp et al., 2004; Nobles
et al., 2013), self-perceived ﬁnancial well-being (Arber et al.,
2014), and subjective perception of income inadequacy (Litwin
and Sapir, 2009). Researchers have suggested that self-perceived
economic well-being is a robust indicator of economic capacity
among elderly adults, and that its role in the health of elderly adults
should not be overlooked (Angel et al., 2003; Litwin and Sapir,
2009; Wada et al., 2002; Zimmerman and Katon, 2005).
Self-perceived economic well-being can affect health through
two main psychosocial mechanisms. First, people with lower
satisfaction are more likely to perceive economic strain, inadequacy, and ﬁnancial insecurity regarding the future (Demakakos
et al., 2008). These unfavorable perceptions may increase anxiety
and the sense of vulnerability, leading to adverse health consequences (Ferraro and Su, 1999; Kahn and Pearlin, 2006; SinghManoux et al., 2003). Second, according to the mechanism of the
reference group theory, by comparing themselves with others,
people feel that their economic resources are insufﬁcient for
participation in the lifestyles or norms (e.g., healthy lifestyles or
behaviors) of their community or peer group; and consequently,
their health is affected (Whelan and Maître, 2013). However, previous studies have focused mainly on the trajectory of income or
wealth rather than considering self-perceived economic satisfaction. Few studies have measured changes in self-perceived economic satisfaction over time among elderly adults. Therefore,
examining the association between changes in economic satisfaction and mortality among elderly adults is crucial.
In addition, it is important to compare the relationship of SES
trajectory and mortality among elderly adults in different societies
because self-perceived economic satisfaction likely varies

according to local social and economic context (Whelan and Maître,
2013). In Taiwan, some elderly people live with and receive ﬁnancial support from their children. Therefore, the economic satisfaction of elderly people may vary according to their dependence on
other people such as their children; the effect of economic satisfaction on health therefore extends beyond the consequence of
absolute material deprivation (Hsu, 2010). The elderly people in
Taiwan commonly assesses their economic satisfaction by
comparing their material recourses and ﬁnancial support or security they receive from their adult children with those of their
reference group. This reﬂects ﬁlial piety attitudes of Confucian
tradition (Whyte, 2004) and is culturally accepted and expected
practice in Taiwan (Lee et al., 1994; Yeh and Bedford, 2003; Yeh
et al., 2013). Therefore, the ﬁndings from the present study can
serve as critical empirical references for policymakers who
distribute health and social welfare resources among elderly adults,
and are particularly relevant to Asian societies. Our study contributed to the limited empirical literature examining the social
mobility model that is based on the patterning and variability of
SES throughout the life course and that predicts mortality among
elderly adults. The objective of this paper was to examine the
relationship between changes in self-perceived economic satisfaction and mortality in later life by using nationally representative
data in Taiwan and adjusting for trajectories of income and wealth.
2. Methods
2.1. Study sample
This study used data from the Survey of Health and Living Status
of the Elderly in Taiwan. The survey was initiated in 1989 and was
designed to collect socioenvironmental, behavioral, living, and
health data from men and women aged 60 years and older (Zimmer
et al., 2005). A three-stage stratiﬁed systematic sampling framework was used in the ﬁrst phase (1989) to obtain a nationally
representative sample. In 1989, 4412 individuals were contacted,
and 4049 individuals were successfully interviewed with a
response rate of 91.8%. The survey sample was nationally representative of the population aged 60 years and older in Taiwan. The
response rate for the second (1993), third (1996), and fourth (1999)
phases were 91.0%, 88.9%, and 90.1%, respectively. The present
study sample comprised 2310 elderly adults for whom SES data
from the four phases (1989, 1993, 1996, and 1999) were available
and who were alive in 1999. The National Cheng Kung University
Human Research Ethics Committee reviewed and approved the
present study.
2.2. Measurements
The primary outcome of interest was the 8-year all-cause mortality, a time (years)-to-death (or censoring) data indicating the
survival years between 1999 and 2007. Information regarding
deaths occurring during the study period was extracted from the
death registration data collected by Taiwan's Ministry of the
Interior.
SES measures included the annual household income, household wealth, and self-perceived economic satisfaction of the respondents. The SES measures observed at four time points (1989,
1993, 1996, and 1999) were used to construct trajectories. At each
survey phase, the household income of the previous year was reported. The annual household income was then divided into
quintiles. The household wealth included three wealth indicators:
(1) possession of investments such as stocks, bonds, mutual funds,
retirement investments, or other investments by the participant or
his or her family (yes, no); (2) possession of a family home by the

M.-C. Lee, N. Huang / Social Science & Medicine 130 (2015) 1e8

participant or his or her spouse (yes, no); and (3) possession of land
or property that was not the primary residence by the participant
or his or her spouse (yes, no). A total wealth score was created by
adding one point for each wealth indicator. Thus, the range of
values for wealth was 0e3, where higher values indicated greater
wealth. Similar household wealth measures have been used in
previous literature (for example, Mete, 2005; Galobardes et al.,
2007). Self-perceived economic satisfaction was a dichotomous

variable determined according to the question, “How do you rate
your household economic status?” (“very satisﬁed,” “satisﬁed,”
“fair,” “dissatisﬁed,” “very dissatisﬁed”). “Very satisﬁed,” “satisﬁed,”
and “fair” were categorized as satisfaction and coded as 1, and
“dissatisﬁed” and “very dissatisﬁed” were categorized as dissatisfaction and coded as 0. These measures have been used previously
in studies of the health effects of SES for elderly adults (Hallqvist
et al., 2004; Pollitt et al., 2005).

Covariates at the baseline phase (1989) were analyzed as potential confounders or mediators of the relationship between SES
and mortality. They include gender (referent male), ethnicity,
marital status, education level, and occupation. Ethnicity was coded
as mainlander Chinese (referent group), Fukianese, Hakka, and
aborigine. Marital status comprised married and others (including
never married, divorced, separated, and widowed (referent group)).
Education was measured using the highest reported level of education attainment and differentiated among none, primary, and
higher than primary (referent group). The 3-category occupational
coding was based on the individual self-reported main occupation
over the life span and differentiated among nonmanual (including
professional, managerial, and administrative occupation (referent
group)), manual, and other (including the unemployed and
housewives/househusbands) occupations. The correlations between self-perceived economic satisfaction and income and between self-perceived economic satisfaction and wealth
signiﬁcantly differed from zero. The Spearman's correlation coefﬁcient for self-perceived economic satisfaction and income was
0.22 (p < 0.0000), and that for self-perceived economic satisfaction
and wealth was 0.08 (p ¼ 0.0003).
2.3. Statistical analyses
SES trajectories were created using the group-based trajectory
modelling approach within the TRAJ macro in Stata/SE V12.0
(Johnson-Lawrence et al., 2013; Nagin, 2005). We ﬁtted the income
and wealth trajectory model to the censored normal distribution,
and used the logit distribution to ascertain self-perceived economic
satisfaction. We used the Bayesian information criterion to determine the appropriate number of groups. For each elderly adult, the
procedure calculated the posterior probability of membership in
each trajectory group, and elderly adults were assigned to the trajectory for which they had the highest probability. All probabilities
were greater than 90%. The resulting category was then included as
a predictor in Cox proportional hazard regression models to
establish the 8-year mortality from 1999 to 2007. Results are presented as hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% conﬁdence intervals (CIs).

4. Discussion
This large population-based study yielded three major ﬁndings.
First, the comparison among the relative sizes of the three SES
trajectory groups indicated that the trajectory patterns between
different SES indicators varied. The majority (73%) of elderly adults
had experienced relatively stable and favorable self-perceived
economic satisfaction; only 15% (354/2310) of respondents had a
decreasing trajectory. However, 32% ((592 þ 155)/2310) and 26%
((300 þ 290)/2310) of respondents exhibited decreasing and
increasing income quintile trajectory and wealth trajectory,
respectively. Income and wealth ﬂuctuated more than selfperceived economic satisfaction did; this result is consistent with
the ﬁndings of previous research (Krieger et al., 1997). The results
empirically demonstrated the strong distinction between three
different SES trajectory patterns among elderly adults and indicated
that neither should be overlooked when investigating the role of
SES mobility in mortality.
Second, the result did not show a stepwise gradient between the
top (consistently high SES) and bottom group (consistently low

SES). The trajectory effects suggested that those with the highest
hazard of mortality were those trajectories that had experienced
proximal low SES at old ages, particularly the wealth trajectory.
Regarding income and wealth, a consistently low SES in early old
age was signiﬁcantly associated with an increased mortality hazard; this result is consistent with ﬁndings regarding Western societies (Johnson-Lawrence et al., 2013). Third, consistently low selfperceived economic satisfaction indicated a higher mortality than
did consistently high self-perceived economic satisfaction. However, unlike income and wealth, no statistical difference was
evident between the group with decreasing and consistently high
economic satisfaction. After we adjusted for income and wealth
trajectories, the self-perceived economic satisfaction trajectory still
exerted a signiﬁcant independent inﬂuence on mortality.
The increased hazards of mortality in elderly adults with
consistently low SES in early old age calculated using income,
wealth, or self-perceived economic satisfaction may have two
plausible interpretations. The risk accumulation model may offer
one explanation for the ﬁndings. The risk accumulation model
posits that the exposure to SES adversity accumulates throughout
the life course and may compromise health in later life (BenShlomo and Kuh, 2002). The ﬁndings of this study are consistent
with those reported in relevant literature suggesting that chronic
poverty is associated with increased mortality (Hallqvist et al.,
2004; Singh-Manoux et al., 2004; Gruenewald et al., 2012), and

and adult life were associated with increased cardiovascular disease mortality (Johnson-Lawrence et al., 2013) and poor physical
performance (Bann et al., 2014). The other plausible explanation
may be the exposure to any poverty early in life (Barker, 1991;
Galobardes et al., 2008). Exposure to low SES earlier in life may
negatively inﬂuence health, and an increasing SES in subsequent
life stages may not offset the negative inﬂuence. Respondents with
income or wealth that increased over time tended to have a relatively low income or wealth level when they were 60 years old. The
ﬁnding of moderately increased mortality associated with the
increasing SES trajectory group may support to the second interpretation, but the increased mortality did not reach the level of
statistical signiﬁcance. Our analyses cannot fully elucidate the inﬂuences of these two different life experiences.
In addition, the adverse inﬂuences that downward income or
wealth mobility in early old age exerted on mortality support social
mobility models. Social mobility models suggest that those exposed
to downward mobility have more accumulated risks compared
with their reference groups, whereas people exposed to upward
mobility are expected to beneﬁt from status improvement
throughout the life course. This result is consistent with ﬁndings
reported in literature (Næss et al., 2006; Niedzwiedz et al., 2012).
However, decreases in income and wealth may be caused by a
decline in health status. Although the additional analyses indicated

This study contributed to related literature because we examined income, wealth, and self-perceived economic satisfaction
trajectories in early old age and their association with all-cause
mortality, demonstrating that the self-perceived economic satisfaction trajectory exerted an independent inﬂuence. This pattern
reﬂected the perceived relative deprivation and the economic
ability to enjoy customary standards of living, which is a critical
factor to health (Subramanyam et al., 2009; Whelan and Maître,
2013). Low satisfaction regarding economic status can reduce social participation and increase the possibility of social exclusion;
both of these phenomena cause stress and are harmful to health
(Chen et al., 2012). Our ﬁndings reinforce the value of analyzing
different measures of SES when studying the etiology of health
inequalities, especially during later life.
This study had three strengths. First, we determined the association between SES trajectories throughout early old age and
mortality by using a representative sample in Taiwan. Second, we
included three SES trajectories (income, wealth, and self-perceived
economic satisfaction trajectories) accounting for different dimensions, and adjusted for cross-sectional educational level and
occupational class. Using three measures of SES facilitated clarifying potential differences in the inﬂuences of SES inequalities on
mortality. Although income and wealth are vital contributors to
mortality inequality among elderly adults, the effect of selfperceived economic satisfaction should not be discounted. Third,
this large population-based study in Taiwan enabled us to obtain
insight into the patterns of SES trajectories and the role of SES
trajectories in the health of elderly adults in Asian societies.
Although self-perceived economic satisfaction was used in this
study, it may not be a perfect measure of a person's perceptions of
SES. Self-perceived economic satisfaction may not adequately
reﬂect a person's sense of position in the social ladder. The measure

7

may also capture the residual of the inadequate measurement of
wealth. Future research with more information on perceptions of
economic statuses may contribute regarding this. As in all longitudinal studies, the attrition occurred because of mortality. Previous analyses of this survey have indicated that one-year mortality
rates increased among respondents with low educational levels
and household incomes (Chen et al., 2007). Because the data used
in this study were limited to those on respondents who were alive
in 1999, analysis may have yielded conservative estimates
regarding the life-course effect on mortality because of health selection. Although the trajectory groups were created using statistical models, the low proportion of decreasing income quintile
trajectories (n ¼ 155, 6.7%) enlarged the CIs of HR estimates. In
addition, the SES measurements may have been subjected to recall
bias.
In summary, data from a large population-based sample of
adults in Taiwan aged 60 years and older indicated that a consistently low SES, irrespective of whether income, wealth, or selfperceived economic satisfaction were considered, was associated
with an increased mortality hazard. The variability associated with
changes in SES over time among the elderly adults, either in terms
of upward or downward mobility in income or wealth, may reﬂect
the differential experiences over this critical period, and its effects
on health could not be fully elucidated in these analyses. However,
the decreasing self-perceived economic satisfaction over this critical period was not found to have an adverse effect on health. The
independent and differential effects of changes in self-perceived
economic satisfaction on mortality should not be overlooked.
Further understanding of the complex interplay and mechanisms
among SES, self-perception of economic wellbeing, and health
among people at early old ages may serve as important empirical
references in the designs and implementation of retirement or
social security policies. Possible adverse health effects of changes in
SES among people at early old ages illustrate the importance of
timely and adequate retirement or social security policies for the
health of elderly adults. Retirement policies or strategies for
maintaining and promoting favorable SES among people in their
early old age may beneﬁt the health of elderly adults later in life.
